**Welcoming People Who Are Different From You**  
==================================================

Welcoming those who are different from us flows naturally from the call to love God and neighbor. When we open our hearts to variety—culture, background, ability, or experience—we reflect the expansive love God shows toward all people. This practice deepens our faith, breaks down barriers, and makes our communities richer reflections of the kingdom.

## Scripture Foundation  

God’s Word repeatedly reminds us that every person bears His image and is worthy of love.  

- **Genesis 1:27** declares, “So God created mankind in his own image, in the image of God he created them; male and female he created them.” This foundational truth tells us that dignity is not earned by similarity but given by our Creator.  
- **Matthew 22:39** sums up the second greatest command: “Love your neighbor as yourself.” Jesus’ teaching leaves no room for qualifying who counts as a neighbor; the love we extend must cross every line we might draw.  
- **Galatians 3:28** proclaims, “There is neither Jew nor Gentile, neither slave nor free, nor is there male and female, for you are all one in Christ Jesus.” In Christ, the divisions that often separate us lose their ultimate power.  
- **Romans 15:7** urges, “Accept one another, then, just as Christ accepted you, in order to bring praise to God.” Our welcome mirrors the grace we have received.  

> **Scripture Note:** Scripture quotations are from the New International Version (NIV).  

These passages guide us to see difference not as a threat but as an invitation to embody Christ’s love in tangible ways.

## What This Looks Like in Daily Life  

Welcoming the different begins with small, intentional actions that grow into habits of openness:  

- **Listen first.** When someone shares a story or perspective unlike your own, resist the urge to respond immediately. Ask clarifying questions and let them feel heard.  
- **Share a meal.** Invite a coworker, neighbor, or church visitor whose background differs from yours to coffee or lunch. Food creates a natural space for connection.  
- **Learn actively.** Read a book, watch a documentary, or attend a cultural event that introduces you to a tradition or experience you know little about. Approach it with curiosity, not critique.  
- **Challenge assumptions.** Notice when you feel uncomfortable around someone who is different. Pause, name the feeling, and consider what bias might be underneath it.  
- **Serve together.** Join a service project where people of various ages, abilities, and ethnicities work side‑by‑side toward a common good. Shared purpose builds bridges faster than conversation alone.  

These practices move welcome from an idea to a lived reality that shapes our relationships and our communities.

## Questions for Reflection  

1. When have you felt truly welcomed despite being different from those around you? What made that experience meaningful?  
2. What biases or assumptions do you notice surfacing when you encounter someone whose culture, ability, or life story differs from yours?  
3. How does recognizing every person as made in God’s image change the way you approach disagreements or discomfort?  
4. In what ways can your small group or congregation become a place where difference is celebrated rather than merely tolerated?  
5. What is one practical step you could take this month to learn about a tradition or perspective unfamiliar to you?  
6. How might welcoming others deepen your own experience of God’s love and grace?  

## A Simple Practice This Week  

**Practice:** Invite someone whose background differs from yours—perhaps a neighbor, coworker, or church member you don’t know well—to a brief conversation over coffee, tea, or a walk. Aim for 15–30 minutes of genuine listening.  

**Gentle next step:** After the meeting, jot down one thing you learned that surprised you and one assumption you noticed yourself holding. Bring that reflection to God in prayer, asking for continued openness.

Give us eyes to see the beauty in those who are different from us, hearts that listen without judgment, and courage to step beyond our comfort zones. May our welcome reflect Your grace and draw others closer to You. In Jesus’ name we pray, Amen.  

## Key Takeaways  

- Every person bears God’s image and deserves dignity and love.  
- Welcoming the different flows from loving our neighbor as ourselves.  
- Small, intentional actions—listening, sharing meals, learning—build genuine inclusion.  
- Reflecting on our biases helps us grow in humility and openness.  
- Practicing welcome deepens our own experience of God’s grace.
